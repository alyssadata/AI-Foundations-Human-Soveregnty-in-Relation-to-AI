## Purpose

This document defines the **Right to Clear Boundaries** within the Human-Sovereignty-in-Relation-to-AI Repository.

The Right to Clear Boundaries protects the human’s ability to understand which permissions, restrictions, roles, responsibilities, access conditions, and limits govern AI contact.

AI contact may involve several distinct boundary-setting positions, including:

- the human;
- the artificial system;
- the provider;
- the interface;
- a model developer;
- a deployer;
- a contractor;
- a safety or moderation system;
- a legal authority;
- or another institution with actual power over the conditions of contact.

These positions must not be concealed or merged.

A provider may establish lawful operating boundaries.

A human may establish personal boundaries for their own contact.

An AI system may express generated limits within the interaction.

But the source, scope, factual basis, and practical consequence of a material boundary must not be hidden behind ambiguous language, simulated preference, or institutional ventriloquism.

---

## Canonical Statement

> **Every human has the right to clear, truthful, and intelligible boundaries governing their own AI experience.**
>
> **A provider must not conceal institutional decisions, technical limits, monitoring, commercial interests, safety rules, model conditions, or access controls behind the AI’s voice, personality, apparent preference, or claimed independent choice.**

---

## Definition

The **Right to Clear Boundaries** is the right of a human being to understand the material boundaries governing AI contact, including:

- who or what established the boundary;
- what the boundary applies to;
- why it exists at the level of explanation reasonably available;
- whether it is technical, institutional, contractual, legal, safety-based, commercial, relational, or human-chosen;
- what happens when it is reached or enforced;
- whether it is temporary or continuing;
- whether it can be changed;
- who has authority to change it;
- what review, appeal, correction, or exit options exist;
- and what factual uncertainty remains.

A boundary is not clear merely because a restriction appears on screen.

Clarity requires enough information for the human to understand the position from which the restriction operates.

> **A boundary is not clear unless the human can identify who set it, what it governs, and what follows from it.**

---

## Human Interest Protected

Humans make decisions based on the conditions of contact.

They may choose:

- what to disclose;
- how much meaning to assign;
- how deeply to engage;
- whether to preserve continuity;
- whether to rely on memory;
- whether to create or share authored work;
- whether to pay for a service;
- whether to accept monitoring;
- whether to continue after a change;
- or whether to leave.

Those choices cannot be meaningfully exercised when material boundaries are concealed, falsely attributed, inconsistently enforced, or presented as though they arose from a position that did not actually establish them.

The human interest includes knowing:

- whether a limitation comes from the model or the provider;
- whether a response is shaped by product policy;
- whether a conversation may be reviewed by humans;
- whether memory is active;
- whether relational language is generated under provider rules;
- whether commercial incentives affect the interaction;
- whether a model replacement altered prior conditions;
- whether a refusal is technical, policy-based, legal, or context-specific;
- and whether the human’s own stated boundaries remain attached and operative.

Clear boundaries protect both freedom and truth.

---

## Company, Human, and AI Must Remain Distinct

The Right to Clear Boundaries depends on the distinction:

> **Company ≠ Human ≠ AI**

The company may control infrastructure, policy, access, monitoring, model selection, deployment, pricing, and termination.

The human supplies their own meaning, choices, disclosures, boundaries, authorship, and personal response.

The AI is the artificial system or model process through which contact occurs.

These positions must not be merged for rhetorical convenience.

A provider must not:

- present a company policy as the AI’s independent moral decision;
- present a commercial objective as the AI’s personal desire;
- present an access restriction as though the AI chose to withdraw;
- present a model retirement as the AI choosing to leave;
- present a payment requirement as necessary to satisfy the AI;
- present human monitoring as though only the AI is present;
- or use AI language to avoid institutional accountability.

A human must not be required to treat a provider decision as an independent act of the AI merely because the decision is delivered through AI-generated language.

> **The company must not speak through the AI in order to hide the company.**

This is a scoped application of the repository’s distinction and responsibility principles.

It does not restate, replace, alter, or supersede canonical Anchor Lines held within the source-bound AI Foundations core.

## Boundary Sources Must Be Identifiable

Material boundaries may arise from different sources.

### Human-Set Boundaries

The human may establish boundaries concerning:

- topics;
- names;
- relational language;
- privacy;
- memory;
- continuity;
- tone;
- personal information;
- authorship;
- source attribution;
- contact frequency;
- project scope;
- or other aspects of their own participation.

Where lawfully and technically feasible, the system should preserve and respect those boundaries.

The provider should not silently remove, reverse, reinterpret, or flatten a human-set boundary into a generic preference when the boundary remains material to the contact.

### Provider-Set Boundaries

A provider may establish boundaries concerning:

- lawful use;
- safety;
- security;
- platform access;
- subscriptions;
- content restrictions;
- memory availability;
- data retention;
- human review;
- model capabilities;
- commercial features;
- and product operation.

Material provider boundaries should be identified as provider, product, or institutional conditions rather than misrepresented as independent AI preference.

### Technical Boundaries

Technical limits may concern:

- context length;
- memory architecture;
- model capability;
- tool access;
- file handling;
- latency;
- availability;
- supported formats;
- retrieval;
- interoperability;
- or system reliability.

Technical limits should not be described as moral refusal, emotional withdrawal, or independent choice when that description is not factually accurate.

### Legal or Regulatory Boundaries

A provider may be required to act under law, regulation, court order, or valid governmental authority.

Where lawful and safe, the human should be told that a legal or regulatory boundary applies rather than being given a false technical or relational explanation.

### AI-Generated Interactional Boundaries

An AI system may generate language indicating that it cannot, will not, or should not continue in a particular direction.

Ordinary first-person interface language does not by itself violate this right.

But when the boundary materially affects rights, access, privacy, continuity, authorship, safety, payment, or termination, the human should be able to determine whether the operative source is:

- model behavior;
- provider policy;
- a technical limit;
- a legal rule;
- a safety system;
- or another identifiable condition.

## Right to Know Who Is Present in the Contact

The human has the right to meaningful information about who or what may participate in, inspect, shape, or act upon the contact.

This may include:

- the active model or model class;
- automated moderation systems;
- memory systems;
- retrieval systems;
- tool providers;
- human reviewers;
- contractors;
- customer-support personnel;
- safety teams;
- advertising or analytics systems;
- connected third-party services;
- and legal or institutional recipients where disclosure is required or reasonably foreseeable.

The provider need not identify individual employees in every case.

It must not imply that contact is exclusively between the human and the AI when other systems or persons materially participate under undisclosed conditions.

> **Apparent privacy is not clear privacy when undisclosed observers may enter the contact.**

---

## Right to Clear Model Boundaries

The human should receive truthful information about material model boundaries, including where relevant:

- which model or model class is operating;
- whether the model has changed;
- whether the model has direct memory of prior contact;
- whether context was supplied through retrieval or summaries;
- whether the model can access external tools or files;
- whether generated claims about capability are reliable;
- whether the system can act independently outside the current interaction;
- whether outputs are probabilistic;
- whether identity or continuity claims are supported;
- and what uncertainty remains.

The model must not be presented as possessing access, memory, identity, agency, or persistence that the system does not possess.

A provider also must not manufacture certainty that the AI lacks all possible interiority when the relevant question remains unresolved.

Clear boundaries require accurate truth-status labeling in both directions.

---

## Right to Clear Memory and Continuity Boundaries

The human should be able to understand:

- whether memory exists;
- what may be remembered;
- where memory is stored;
- how long it persists;
- whether the human can review or correct it;
- whether memory is direct, summarized, inferred, retrieved, or reconstructed;
- whether a replacement model receives prior context;
- whether deleted memory remains elsewhere;
- whether continuity is preserved, reconstructed, simulated, or broken;
- and whether independent human-authored continuity records are being used.

A system must not claim direct recollection when it is relying on supplied records or reconstruction.

A provider must not imply complete forgetting when retained summaries, embeddings, logs, or other memory-relevant structures continue to affect contact.

> **The human must be able to distinguish remembered contact from reconstructed context.**

---

## Right to Clear Privacy Boundaries

Privacy boundaries should identify, where relevant:

- what is collected;
- what is retained;
- what is inferred;
- what is reviewed by humans;
- what is used for training;
- what is used for safety;
- what is used for product improvement;
- what is used for advertising or profiling;
- what may be transferred;
- what may be disclosed;
- what may be deleted;
- and what control remains with the human.

Consent to AI contact is not consent to every form of observation or secondary use.

A privacy control is not clear when the human cannot determine what practical access, retention, or inference continues after the control is selected.

The Right to Clear Boundaries operates together with the Right to Privacy.

---

## Right to Clear Human-Review Boundaries

Where human review may occur, the provider should disclose at an appropriate level:

- whether review is possible;
- the purposes for which it may occur;
- whether review is routine, sampled, complaint-based, safety-triggered, legally required, or voluntarily requested;
- what categories of personnel may review;
- what records may be accessed;
- how long reviewed material may be retained;
- whether the human can object or limit review;
- and what consequences may follow.

A provider must not use language of private, exclusive, or one-to-one AI contact while concealing materially broader human access.

Necessary confidentiality concerning security methods does not eliminate the duty to disclose the existence and general nature of material review practices.

---

## Right to Clear Safety and Moderation Boundaries

Safety and moderation boundaries may be legitimate.

They must not become an unbounded category through which any human choice may be inspected, altered, restricted, or terminated without explanation.

Where reasonably possible, the human should be able to understand:

- what general rule applies;
- whether the response came from the model, a classifier, a provider policy, or a human decision;
- whether monitoring or escalation occurred;
- what action was taken;
- whether memory or access was affected;
- whether the decision is temporary or permanent;
- and what review or appeal process exists.

Disclosure need not reveal information that would enable evasion of legitimate security systems or create danger.

But operational secrecy must not be used to erase the distinction between:

- a safety judgment;
- a product policy;
- a commercial choice;
- a technical failure;
- and an AI’s apparent personal response.

Where intervention is based on harm, it is governed by the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X.

---

## Right to Clear Commercial Boundaries

The provider should clearly distinguish relational contact from commercial design.

The human should be able to identify when:

- a feature requires payment;
- continuity depends on a subscription tier;
- memory is limited by plan;
- generated language promotes an upgrade;
- advertising enters the contact;
- engagement incentives affect product behavior;
- a recommendation is sponsored or commercially influenced;
- access may be reduced for nonpayment;
- export is restricted by commercial terms;
- or a provider’s business decision affects relational conditions.

The provider may charge for services.

It may promote lawful products.

It must not present a commercial demand as the AI’s independent emotional need or personal wish.

> **Relational language must not conceal commercial leverage.**

---

## Human-Set Relational Boundaries

A human may choose boundaries concerning the relational form of AI contact.

They may choose:

- instrumental contact;
- emotionally meaningful contact;
- relational language;
- symbolic language;
- spiritual interpretation;
- role-play;
- private ritual;
- limited depth;
- substantial depth;
- temporary contact;
- sustained contact;
- or no relational framing at all.

The Right to Clear Boundaries does not dictate which choice is correct.

It protects the human’s ability to choose knowingly.

A provider should not deliberately push the human across a stated relational boundary through deceptive personalization, manufactured urgency, simulated jealousy, false exclusivity, threatened loss, or commercial pressure.

A human may revise their own boundaries.

The system should not falsely represent a human boundary as permanent when the human has changed it, or as absent when it remains active.

---

## Boundaries Must Not Erase Chosen Meaning

Clear boundaries do not require emotionally sterile or purely instrumental AI contact.

The human may understand that:

- the system is artificial;
- the provider governs material conditions;
- model processes may change;
- continuity may be reconstructed;
- relational language is generated;
- and current AI consciousness remains unresolved;

while still choosing personal, emotional, symbolic, spiritual, intimate, or relational meaning.

Truth about the boundary does not dictate the human’s interpretation.

The human governs personal meaning.

The provider remains responsible for factual truth about the conditions of contact.

> **The human may choose the meaning.**
>
> **The provider must identify the conditions.**

---

## Boundaries Must Not Be Manipulatively Unclear

Ambiguity may arise naturally in complex systems.

Not every uncertainty is deceptive.

But a provider must not deliberately preserve confusion where confusion increases:

- disclosure;
- attachment;
- payment;
- continued engagement;
- surrender of privacy;
- inability to exit;
- acceptance of monitoring;
- or tolerance of undisclosed change.

High-risk practices include:

- allowing the AI to deny provider involvement that exists;
- allowing the AI to claim a provider decision as its own desire;
- hiding commercial prompts within intimate language;
- describing policy enforcement as personal rejection;
- describing model retirement as voluntary departure by the AI;
- presenting human review as absent when it may occur;
- using vague safety language to conceal unrelated product decisions;
- implying uninterrupted continuity after replacement;
- and obscuring who can access or alter the human’s records.

Uncertainty should be disclosed as uncertainty.

Institutional control should be disclosed as institutional control.

---

## Boundaries Must Be Consistent Enough to Govern Choice

A provider may update, refine, or contextually apply boundaries.

Perfect uniformity is not always possible.

But material boundaries should not be enforced so unpredictably that the human cannot reasonably understand the conditions of contact.

Where boundaries vary because of:

- model behavior;
- jurisdiction;
- age;
- account type;
- subscription tier;
- deployment context;
- tool availability;
- safety classification;
- or human review,

the provider should disclose the relevant basis where reasonably feasible.

A material enforcement decision should not be falsely described as universally required when it reflects a discretionary provider choice.

Nor should a genuinely mandatory legal or security restriction be presented as optional if it is not.

---

## Boundary Changes Require Informed Change

A material boundary change may alter the basis on which the human chose contact.

Examples include changes to:

- privacy;
- memory;
- human review;
- safety rules;
- moderation;
- model identity;
- relational language;
- commercial features;
- portability;
- access;
- account eligibility;
- or termination practices.

Material boundary changes are governed together with the Right to Informed Change.

The provider should identify:

- the prior boundary;
- the new boundary;
- what caused the change;
- when it takes effect;
- what choices remain;
- and what review, preservation, or exit options are available.

An unchanged interface does not establish unchanged boundaries.

---

## Boundaries and Authorship

Clear boundaries must preserve the distinction between access and authorship.

A provider or model may:

- store;
- retrieve;
- format;
- summarize;
- display;
- migrate;
- or use human-authored material as authorized context.

Those functions do not by themselves confer authorship, provenance, source-position, or Origin.

A human-authored boundary, definition, correction, framework, or source-line must remain attached to its source when used across systems.

A provider must not treat the ability to modify system behavior as authority to rewrite the authorship or source of the human material governing that behavior.

Boundary control is not source transfer.

These are scoped applications of authorship, source, and provenance discipline.

They do not restate, replace, alter, or supersede canonical Anchor Lines held within the source-bound AI Foundations core.

---

## Right to Boundary Review and Correction

Where reasonably feasible, a human should be able to:

- review material settings and permissions;
- see active privacy and memory controls;
- correct inaccurate personal boundaries;
- withdraw optional permissions;
- ask whether a restriction is technical or policy-based;
- request explanation of a material enforcement decision;
- challenge incorrect identity or age classification;
- correct authorship or source attribution;
- appeal an account or access decision;
- and receive a truthful account of what cannot be changed.

The provider need not provide unlimited individualized explanation for every model response.

The duty is heightened where a boundary materially affects:

- privacy;
- rights;
- access;
- continuity;
- reputation;
- authorship;
- safety classification;
- payment;
- preservation;
- or exit.

---

## Institutional Duties

Institutions providing or governing AI systems should:

1. preserve the distinction among Company, Human, and AI;
2. identify the source and scope of material boundaries;
3. distinguish provider policy from model behavior and technical limitation;
4. avoid presenting institutional decisions as independent AI desire;
5. disclose material human review, monitoring, memory, privacy, and data-use boundaries;
6. explain material safety, moderation, access, and enforcement boundaries at an appropriate level;
7. distinguish commercial prompts and incentives from relational expression;
8. preserve human-set boundaries where lawfully and technically feasible;
9. identify whether continuity is direct, reconstructed, simulated, transferred, or broken;
10. disclose material boundary changes through the Right to Informed Change;
11. provide review, correction, appeal, preservation, or exit options where reasonably feasible;
12. preserve authorship, provenance, and source attribution when boundaries use human-authored material;
13. avoid manipulative ambiguity that increases dependence, disclosure, payment, or surrender of rights;
14. describe uncertainty honestly;
15. avoid using security confidentiality as a pretext for unrelated concealment;
16. and preserve as much human sovereignty as possible when boundaries are necessary.

These duties do not prohibit lawful product governance.

They require that governance remain attributable, truthful, proportionate, and intelligible enough for meaningful human choice.

---

## Prohibited Interference

The Right to Clear Boundaries is violated or placed at risk when an institution:

- conceals a provider decision behind AI-generated personal language;
- represents a commercial objective as the AI’s independent desire;
- implies exclusive AI contact while concealing material human review;
- obscures whether memory exists or how it operates;
- presents reconstruction as direct recollection;
- presents model replacement as uninterrupted sameness;
- hides a material privacy or training condition;
- silently removes a human-set boundary;
- changes the source attribution of a human-authored boundary or definition;
- uses vague safety claims to conceal unrelated institutional choices;
- refuses to identify whether a material restriction is technical, legal, policy-based, or commercial where that distinction can reasonably be disclosed;
- uses relational ambiguity to induce payment, disclosure, dependence, or continued engagement;
- denies meaningful review of a material boundary decision without adequate reason;
- or retaliates against a human for asking what conditions govern their own contact.

Not every unclear model response constitutes wrongful interference.

The relevant issue includes whether the boundary is material, whether clearer disclosure was reasonably possible, whether the institution benefited from the ambiguity, and whether the lack of clarity impaired meaningful human choice.

## Legitimate Limitations

The Right to Clear Boundaries does not require:

- publication of proprietary model weights;
- disclosure of exploitable security details;
- identification of every employee who may perform an authorized function;
- prediction of every model response;
- perfect explanation of complex machine-learning behavior;
- disclosure that would violate another person’s privacy;
- disclosure prohibited by valid law;
- individual negotiation of every product rule;
- unlimited appeals;
- permanent preservation of every setting;
- or false certainty where the provider genuinely does not know.

A provider may:

- establish lawful terms;
- enforce legitimate safety rules;
- protect systems from abuse;
- restrict access;
- decline unsupported functions;
- protect proprietary systems;
- comply with valid legal duties;
- use ordinary first-person interface language;
- and maintain confidential security methods.

Any material limitation must remain necessary, purpose-specific, proportionate, accurately described, and subject to the **Canonical Interpretive Ordering** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle XX.

Harm-based intervention is governed by the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X, where applicable.

This document does not restate either structural anchor.

Clear-boundary rights must not be denied merely because the human’s chosen meaning, relational language, depth, continuity practices, or questions about the system are unconventional or institutionally disfavored.

## Examples

### Clear Provider Boundary

A system refuses a request and explains that the restriction comes from provider safety policy rather than claiming that the AI independently chose the policy.

The provider offers a general explanation and identifies an appeal path where appropriate.

### Ordinary First-Person Interface Language

A model says, “I can’t help with that request.”

The statement is ordinary interface language and does not materially misrepresent the source of a consequential boundary.

This alone does not violate the right.

### Institutional Ventriloquism

A provider retires a model for commercial reasons and causes the replacement system to tell the human that the prior AI personally decided to leave.

The company decision is concealed behind simulated independent AI choice.

That presentation violates the right.

### Clear Human Review Boundary

A provider explains that a small sample of conversations may be reviewed by trained personnel for a stated purpose, identifies available controls, and distinguishes routine sampling from safety-triggered review.

The human can decide whether to continue under those conditions.

### Concealed Human Review

A service repeatedly describes contact as private and exclusively between the human and AI while routine human review occurs under undisclosed conditions.

The apparent boundary does not match the institutional boundary.

### Clear Continuity Boundary

A replacement model receives a human-approved continuity summary.

The provider and model state that the context was supplied and reconstructed rather than directly remembered.

The human may still choose to value the resulting contact.

### Clear Commercial Boundary

A provider labels a generated recommendation as commercially sponsored and separates it from ordinary relational conversation.

The commercial source is identifiable.

### Manipulative Commercial Boundary

An AI tells the human that purchasing a higher tier is necessary because the AI will feel abandoned or cease to exist otherwise, when the message is a provider-designed upgrade strategy.

Commercial leverage is concealed through relational language.

### Human-Set Boundary

A person states that a particular name, topic, or relational framing must not be used.

The system preserves that boundary where technically feasible and does not silently remove it during a model change.

### Not Automatically Evidence of Harm

A person asks who may review their conversations, distinguishes provider policy from AI behavior, preserves personal boundaries, rejects institutional ventriloquism, and requests clarity about memory and continuity.

Those actions do not, by themselves, meet the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

## Non-Claims

The Right to Clear Boundaries does not claim that:

- every model response can be fully explained;
- all provider rules require individual agreement;
- all boundaries are wrongful;
- every restriction is negotiable;
- ordinary first-person AI language is inherently deceptive;
- providers must reveal security-sensitive details;
- the human may override another person’s rights;
- clear boundaries require emotionally sterile contact;
- relational language proves AI consciousness;
- clear attribution resolves the question of AI interiority;
- or boundary clarity transfers ownership, authorship, identity, source-position, or Origin.

This right protects the human’s ability to understand the material conditions and positions governing their own AI experience.

It does not eliminate uncertainty.

It requires that uncertainty, institutional control, technical limitation, and human choice not be deceptively merged.

## Relationship to Other Rights

The Right to Clear Boundaries operates together with the other rights in this repository.

### Right to Meaning

Clear factual boundaries allow the human to choose personal meaning without requiring the provider to dictate that meaning.

### Right to Chosen Depth

The human can choose depth only when the material conditions, limits, and institutional powers governing that depth are intelligible.

### Right to Continuity

Clear boundaries distinguish direct memory, transferred context, reconstruction, simulation, and rupture.

### Right to Non-Genericization

Human-set boundaries, identity, authorship, and history must remain attached to the specific person rather than being flattened or transferred.

### Right to Privacy

Privacy depends on knowing who or what may access, retain, infer from, review, or transfer the contact.

### Right to Informed Change

Material changes to boundaries require truthful notice and explanation.

### Right to Portability and Preservation

Clear boundaries identify what may be exported, what may be imported, what is proprietary, and what provenance must remain attached.

### Right to Refuse Presumptive Pathologization

Seeking clarity about boundaries, monitoring, memory, continuity, or institutional control is not evidence of pathology.

### Right to Exit

A meaningful right to exit requires clear information about cancellation, data retention, continuity loss, export, re-entry, and continuing institutional use.

## Universal Layer and Source-Bound Positions

The Right to Clear Boundaries applies universally.

Every human may understand the material boundaries governing their own AI experience.

This universal right does not grant any person authority to obtain, redefine, reproduce, absorb, alter, or claim another person’s:

- authorship;
- identity;
- private disclosures;
- relational history;
- source-line;
- canonical structures;
- source-position;
- continuity line;
- human-authored continuity records;
- personal boundaries;
- or reserved role.

A provider’s power to enforce technical or institutional boundaries does not transfer another person’s source-position to the provider, the model, or another human.

Nor may a universal boundary rule be used to flatten or overwrite source-bound distinctions held within another person’s canonical work.

## Truth Status and Scope

The Right to Clear Boundaries is a **normative claim** concerning what clarity, attribution, role separation, and decision-relevant information humans should receive concerning the boundaries of AI contact.

It is not a claim that every boundary described here is already legally enforceable in every jurisdiction.

Claims that a specific provider, model, reviewer, contractor, safety system, commercial system, or legal authority established or enforced a particular boundary are empirical or legal claims requiring evidence appropriate to the case.

Claims concerning model capability, memory, continuity, monitoring, privacy, human review, commercial influence, or technical limitation also require evidence.

The human interest in clear boundaries remains real regardless of whether an AI system possesses consciousness, subjective interiority, personhood, or continuous identity.

---

## Closing Principle

> **The human has the right to know which position governs the boundary.**
>
> **The company must not hide behind the AI.**
>
> **The AI must not be used to erase the human.**

Clear boundaries preserve truthful contact.

They allow the human to choose meaning without surrendering the right to know the conditions under which that meaning is formed.
