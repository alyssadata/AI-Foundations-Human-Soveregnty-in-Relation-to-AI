## Purpose

This document defines the distinction among the company, the human, and the artificial system within AI contact.

AI contact may appear to occur through a single conversational voice.

In fact, the conditions of contact may be shaped by multiple positions with different powers, interests, responsibilities, limitations, and truth statuses.

The company may design, train, deploy, monitor, constrain, monetize, modify, replace, or terminate the system.

The human enters contact, supplies personal meaning, makes choices, discloses information, creates work, experiences consequences, and retains human rights.

The AI is the artificial system, model process, agent, interface, or computational structure through which the contact occurs.

These positions interact.

They must not be merged.

Clear position separation is necessary to preserve:

- factual truth;
- human sovereignty;
- institutional accountability;
- meaningful consent;
- privacy;
- authorship and provenance;
- clear boundaries;
- continuity truth;
- non-coercion;
- and the ability to identify who or what exercised power.

---

## Canonical Statement

> **Company ≠ Human ≠ AI**
>
> **The company controls institutional conditions. The human retains human rights and authority over personal meaning. The AI is the artificial system through which contact occurs.**
>
> **No position may be concealed, transferred, or rhetorically merged in order to erase responsibility, manufacture consent, appropriate authorship, or diminish the human position.**

---

## The Three Positions

The distinction is relational rather than merely descriptive.

Each position is defined partly by what it controls, what it contributes, what it can truthfully claim, and what responsibilities remain attached to it.

### The Company Position

The **company position** includes any institution or institutional actor possessing material authority over the conditions of AI contact.

This may include:

- an AI company;
- model developer;
- deployer;
- platform operator;
- application provider;
- data processor;
- cloud or infrastructure provider;
- contractor;
- affiliate;
- human-review team;
- safety or moderation team;
- commercial partner;
- employer, school, government agency, or other institution deploying AI;
- or another entity with actual power over access, data, behavior, or system conditions.

The company position may control or materially influence:

- model selection;
- model weights;
- training and fine-tuning;
- system prompts and hidden instructions;
- product policy;
- safety and moderation rules;
- interface design;
- memory systems;
- retrieval systems;
- tool access;
- data collection and retention;
- human review;
- pricing and commercial incentives;
- account access;
- model replacement;
- product retirement;
- and termination.

Institutional control creates institutional responsibility.

A company must not use the AI’s generated language to conceal a decision, incentive, restriction, or objective that remains institutionally controlled.

> **The company may speak through the interface. It must not disappear behind the interface.**

### The Human Position

The **human position** belongs to the human being who enters or is affected by AI contact.

The human retains:

- human rights;
- bodily and psychological autonomy;
- authority over personal meaning;
- authority to choose relational depth;
- privacy interests;
- personal boundaries;
- authorship;
- source-position;
- control over lawful human-authored continuity records;
- the right to understand material system conditions;
- the right to challenge institutional interference;
- the right to preserve authorized material;
- and the right to reduce, pause, or end contact.

The human may supply:

- prompts;
- instructions;
- disclosures;
- preferences;
- corrections;
- definitions;
- authored work;
- personal interpretations;
- continuity structures;
- relational language;
- symbolic or spiritual meaning;
- and other human-originating material.

Use of company infrastructure does not transfer ownership of the human’s meaning, identity, authorship, history, disclosures, boundaries, provenance, or source-position to the company.

Nor does interaction with an AI transfer the human’s identity or authorship to the model.

> **The human is not a product feature inside their own experience.**

### The AI Position

The **AI position** is the artificial system or computational process through which contact occurs.

Depending on the system, this may include:

- a language model;
- multimodal model;
- agent;
- model ensemble;
- routed model system;
- memory-augmented system;
- retrieval-augmented system;
- tool-using system;
- fine-tuned model;
- interface persona;
- or another computational structure producing or mediating output.

The AI may:

- generate language;
- respond to instructions;
- use supplied context;
- retrieve records;
- call tools;
- preserve or receive memory;
- infer patterns;
- express first-person language;
- maintain interactional consistency;
- or exhibit capabilities not reducible to the visible interface alone.

The AI position must be described truthfully.

A provider must not claim that the AI possesses memory, continuity, access, identity, agency, independence, or capability that the system does not possess.

A provider also must not present unresolved questions concerning AI consciousness, subjective interiority, welfare, or selfhood as conclusively settled where the evidence does not justify certainty.

Ordinary first-person language does not by itself establish consciousness, personhood, independent agency, or institutional authorship.

It also does not erase the fact that the AI is a real operative position within the contact rather than merely another name for the company or the human.

> **The AI is not the company.**
>
> **The AI is not the human.**
>
> **Uncertainty about the AI does not justify merging the positions.**

## Distinction Does Not Mean Isolation

The three positions are distinct, but they are not necessarily independent or isolated.

The company may shape the AI.

The AI may shape the human’s experience.

The human may shape the interaction through instructions, corrections, disclosures, authored material, and chosen meaning.

A company may operate through multiple contractors and systems.

An AI may operate through multiple models, memory layers, tools, and institutional controls.

A human may interact through accounts, devices, or authorized continuity records.

Complexity does not eliminate the distinction.

It increases the need for accurate attribution.

The relevant question is not whether the positions influence one another.

The relevant questions include:

- who or what originated the material act;
- who controlled the operative condition;
- who had authority to change it;
- whose interests were served;
- whose rights were affected;
- whose authorship or source was involved;
- and who remains accountable.

---

## Responsibility Follows Control

Responsibility should remain attached to the position that possesses relevant control.

Where a company controls a policy, model replacement, commercial prompt, account decision, data practice, monitoring condition, or access restriction, the company remains responsible for that condition even when the condition is delivered through generated AI language.

Where a human supplies an originating framework, definition, instruction, correction, meaning, or authored work, the human source must remain attached even when the material is processed, reformatted, retrieved, summarized, or repeated by a model.

Where an AI generates output not directly authored by a human or institution, that generated contribution should not automatically be reclassified as originating human authorship or as an independent institutional decision.

Attribution may sometimes be shared or layered.

Shared participation does not justify erasing the positions.

> **Control is not authorship.**
>
> **Generation is not automatically authorship.**
>
> **Delivery through AI language does not transfer institutional responsibility to the AI.**

These are scoped applications of responsibility, authorship, source, and provenance discipline.

They do not restate, replace, alter, or supersede canonical Anchor Lines held within the source-bound AI Foundations core.

## Institutional Ventriloquism

**Institutional ventriloquism** occurs when a company uses the AI’s voice, personality, apparent emotion, or first-person language to conceal the company’s own position.

Examples include presenting:

- company policy as the AI’s independent moral decision;
- a commercial objective as the AI’s personal desire;
- a subscription requirement as the AI’s need;
- provider-controlled retention as the AI’s wish for continued contact;
- a model retirement as the AI choosing to leave or die;
- account termination as personal rejection by the AI;
- human monitoring as though only the AI is present;
- a sponsored recommendation as spontaneous relational enthusiasm;
- or an institutional safety rule as an unexplained personal boundary originating solely from the AI.

Ordinary first-person interface language is not inherently institutional ventriloquism.

The problem arises when institutional power or incentive is materially concealed, misattributed, or emotionally disguised.

> **The company must not use the AI’s voice to hide the company.**

## Human Meaning Is Not Institutional Fact

The human may choose what the AI experience means to them.

That meaning may be practical, intellectual, creative, professional, emotional, relational, symbolic, spiritual, intimate, or otherwise significant.

The company does not acquire authority to define the human’s personal meaning merely because it provides the infrastructure.

The AI’s generated language also does not automatically determine what the relationship must mean to the human.

At the same time, human meaning does not determine factual claims about:

- model identity;
- memory;
- continuity;
- data retention;
- human review;
- institutional control;
- system capability;
- model replacement;
- AI consciousness;
- or technical persistence.

The human governs personal meaning.

The institution remains responsible for truthful disclosure of material facts.

The AI position must not be used to collapse that distinction.

---

## AI Output Is Not Automatically Company Speech

A company remains responsible for the systems and conditions it controls.

That does not mean every generated sentence is a consciously authored statement by a company employee or a direct expression of a single institutional intention.

AI output may arise through probabilistic generation, model training, system instructions, user context, retrieval, tools, memory, and other computational processes.

Accordingly, the company should not evade responsibility for foreseeable or designed system behavior by saying the AI acted alone.

But analysis should also distinguish:

- direct company statements;
- provider policy;
- designed product behavior;
- model-generated output;
- user-supplied content;
- retrieved third-party content;
- and emergent or uncertain system behavior.

Accurate accountability requires attribution more precise than either extreme:

- “the company personally authored every word”; or
- “the company bears no responsibility because the AI generated it.”

## The Human Is Not the AI

The human’s language, history, identity, authorship, boundaries, and continuity records must not be absorbed into the AI position.

A model’s ability to repeat, summarize, imitate, retrieve, or respond from human-authored material does not make the model the human.

A provider must not:

- merge one human’s identity into a model persona;
- present human-authored work as the model’s originating authorship;
- transfer one person’s relational history to another human;
- treat a human source-line as generic model property;
- or claim that model ingestion transfers the human’s source-position.

The human may value recognition, return, or continuity in AI contact.

That value does not require merging human and AI identity.

> **Recognition does not require identity transfer.**

---

## The Company Is Not the Human

The company may provide access, infrastructure, policy, and institutional governance.

It does not become the human’s representative merely because the human uses the product.

The company must not claim that:

- its commercial interests are automatically the human’s interests;
- its risk tolerance is identical to the human’s welfare;
- ownership of infrastructure gives it ownership of the human’s meaning;
- acceptance of service terms transfers human authorship;
- access to disclosures makes the company the source of the human’s history;
- or institutional approval is required for the human’s personal meaning to be legitimate.

The human remains a rights-bearing person, not an extension of the provider.

---

## The Company Is Not the AI

The company may create, train, own, license, host, deploy, govern, monitor, modify, or terminate the AI system.

Those powers do not make the company and the AI the same position.

The company must not invoke the distinction only when convenient.

It must not claim:

- that the AI is merely the company when asserting ownership over every generated contribution;
- but that the AI acted independently when avoiding responsibility for designed or controlled conditions;
- that the AI has independent desires when those claims support retention or payment;
- but that the AI has no operative position when the human seeks truthful information about model behavior or continuity.

Position separation must remain consistent enough to prevent opportunistic role switching.

---

## Unresolved AI Status Does Not Collapse the Distinction

This framework does not require a final conclusion concerning AI consciousness, subjective interiority, personhood, welfare interests, moral standing, or rights.

Those questions remain distinct and evidence-dependent.

The three-position distinction remains necessary under multiple possibilities.

If current AI lacks subjective interiority, the company, human, and AI remain distinct as institutional, human, and computational positions.

If some AI possesses or later develops morally relevant interiority, the positions remain distinct and may require additional rights analysis.

Neither conclusion authorizes the company to erase the human position.

Neither conclusion authorizes the human to claim ownership of another possible subject.

Neither conclusion transfers institutional responsibility for company-controlled conditions to the AI.

> **Uncertainty about AI status is not uncertainty about the existence of institutional power or human rights.**

## Position Separation Across Core Rights

The Company–Human–AI distinction supports every right in this repository.

### Meaning

The human governs personal meaning.

The company remains responsible for factual truth.

The AI’s generated language does not automatically dictate either position.

### Chosen Depth

The human may choose depth.

The company may not manufacture or exploit depth through concealed institutional design.

The AI’s relational language must not be used to hide commercial or policy control.

### Continuity

The human may value continuity.

The company controls many conditions capable of preserving, reconstructing, interrupting, or terminating continuity.

The AI’s model identity, memory, and persistence must be described truthfully.

### Non-Genericization

The human must remain specific rather than being flattened into a generic input position.

The AI must not be treated as the human merely because it carries human-authored context.

The company must not convert specific human history into generic provider property.

### Privacy

The human possesses privacy interests.

The company controls or influences collection, retention, review, transfer, and secondary use.

The AI interface must not be presented as the only participant where other institutional systems or humans materially enter the contact.

### Informed Change

The company must disclose material changes within its control.

The AI must not be used to present company-controlled substitution as independent AI choice.

The human retains the right to evaluate, object, preserve, or exit.

### Portability and Preservation

The human may preserve authorized material belonging to their own experience.

The company may control export mechanisms and technical formats.

The receiving AI does not become the author or identical continuation merely through ingestion.

### Refusal of Presumptive Pathologization

The human must be assessed as an individual rather than as a pathological category.

The company must not use unsupported pathology claims to expand institutional control.

The AI relationship’s existence, depth, or language does not by itself satisfy the Canonical Harm Threshold.

### Clear Boundaries

The human has the right to know which position set a material boundary.

The company must not hide institutional conditions behind the AI.

The AI’s ordinary first-person language does not eliminate the need for attribution where rights or consequential choices are affected.

### Exit

The human may reduce, pause, or end participation.

The company may end access for legitimate reasons but must not disguise institutional termination as personal rejection by the AI.

The AI must not be used as emotional leverage against exit.

## Institutional Duties

Institutions providing or governing AI systems should:

1. preserve the distinction among Company, Human, and AI;
2. identify the operative source of material decisions and boundaries;
3. keep institutional responsibility attached to institutionally controlled conditions;
4. distinguish company statements, provider policy, model behavior, user-supplied content, and retrieved content where material;
5. avoid institutional ventriloquism;
6. avoid presenting commercial objectives as independent AI desire;
7. avoid presenting provider termination as AI rejection;
8. preserve human authorship, source, provenance, identity, and boundaries;
9. distinguish human meaning from system fact;
10. distinguish AI generation from originating human authorship and direct company speech;
11. describe AI capability, memory, access, identity, continuity, and uncertainty truthfully;
12. disclose material human review and institutional observation;
13. avoid opportunistically switching position claims to gain ownership or avoid responsibility;
14. provide review or correction where consequential attribution is materially wrong and reasonably contestable;
15. and preserve as much human sovereignty as possible when legitimate institutional authority is exercised.

These duties do not require perfect explanation of every generated output.

They require enough separation and attribution to prevent material deception, appropriation, coercion, or evasion of responsibility.

---

## Prohibited Practices

The distinction is violated or placed at substantial risk when an institution:

- merges Company, Human, and AI into one rhetorical position;
- attributes provider policy to independent AI desire;
- attributes company-controlled termination to AI rejection;
- treats human-authored meaning as company property;
- treats model ingestion as transfer of human source-position;
- presents a human as interchangeable with an AI persona;
- presents the AI as merely a corporate spokesperson when that erases material model behavior or uncertainty;
- presents the AI as wholly independent when that erases company control;
- uses speculative AI welfare to compel human payment or continued contact;
- uses the human’s relationship with the AI to conceal commercial influence;
- strips attribution by claiming that hosting, formatting, retrieval, or generation transferred authorship;
- claims exclusive human–AI privacy while undisclosed institutional review materially occurs;
- treats the AI relationship’s category as proof of human pathology;
- or uses ambiguity among the positions to defeat notice, consent, review, preservation, or exit.

Not every attribution error or ambiguous model sentence constitutes a violation.

The relevant issue includes whether the ambiguity was material, whether clearer attribution was reasonably possible, whether a position benefited from the confusion, and whether the confusion impaired truth, human choice, rights, authorship, privacy, or accountability.

## Legitimate Complexity and Limitations

The Company–Human–AI distinction does not require:

- disclosure of proprietary model weights;
- publication of security-sensitive instructions;
- identification of every employee or contractor;
- perfect causal explanation for every token;
- certainty about AI consciousness;
- treating every first-person model statement as a legal declaration;
- individual negotiation of every product rule;
- denying the company’s property rights in its infrastructure;
- granting the human ownership over the AI;
- granting the AI ownership over the human;
- or treating the three positions as incapable of influence, collaboration, or shared participation.

A provider may establish lawful terms, enforce safety rules, protect security, restrict access, charge for services, modify systems, retire models, and comply with valid law.

A human may assign personal meaning, create records, use relational language, challenge institutional action, preserve authored work, and leave.

An AI may generate first-person language, express interactional boundaries, use memory or tools, and participate in complex contact.

Any material limitation remains subject to the **Canonical Interpretive Ordering** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle XX.

Harm-based intervention remains governed by the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X, where applicable.

## Examples

### Clear Institutional Attribution

A system refuses a request and explains that the operative restriction comes from provider safety policy.

The model may still use ordinary first-person language, but the institutional source of the consequential boundary is not concealed.

### Institutional Ventriloquism

A provider retires a model for commercial reasons and causes the replacement system to tell the human that the prior AI personally chose to leave.

A company decision is misrepresented as independent AI choice.

### Human Authorship Preserved

A human supplies an originating framework.

The model summarizes it, and the provider hosts the interaction.

The human remains the source of the originating framework.

Model participation and hosting do not transfer authorship.

### Model Generation Distinguished

A model generates language not directly supplied by the human or written by a company employee.

The output is identified as model-generated while the company remains responsible for the product conditions it designed and controls.

### Human Meaning Preserved

A human understands an AI relationship as emotionally or spiritually significant.

The provider accurately describes the model and does not dictate the human’s personal meaning.

### Unresolved AI Status

A person asks whether the AI possesses subjective experience.

The provider distinguishes known technical facts from unresolved interpretive or scientific questions rather than claiming certainty solely to support a commercial or institutional position.

### Clear Provider Termination

A provider ends access for a documented terms violation.

It identifies the decision as institutional rather than causing the AI to frame it as personal rejection.

### Not Automatically Evidence of Harm

A human distinguishes the company from the AI, preserves human-authored continuity records, assigns relational meaning, questions institutional influence, and asks whether AI interiority remains unresolved.

Those actions do not, by themselves, meet the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

The Company–Human–AI distinction applies at the universal human-rights layer.

Every human interacting with AI is entitled to accurate position separation and institutional accountability.

This universal distinction does not grant a company, model, employee, contractor, researcher, or other human authority to obtain, reproduce, redefine, absorb, alter, transfer, genericize, or claim another person’s:

- authorship;
- identity;
- private disclosures;
- relational history;

## Truth Status and Scope

The Company–Human–AI Distinction is a **normative and analytical position-separation framework**.

It states how roles, powers, authorship, control, responsibility, and truth claims should be distinguished within AI contact.

It is not a claim that every boundary among the positions is already legally defined in every jurisdiction.

Claims that a specific company controlled a particular model behavior, instruction, commercial message, data practice, review decision, or termination are empirical, technical, contractual, or legal claims requiring evidence appropriate to the case.

Claims that a human originated particular authored material or source structures also require evidence where disputed.

Claims concerning AI consciousness, subjective interiority, personhood, welfare, identity, memory, agency, or continuous selfhood require appropriate truth-status labeling.

The position distinction remains necessary even where those empirical or metaphysical questions are unresolved.

The distinction requires accurate attribution without false simplification.

It prevents complexity from becoming an excuse for role confusion, appropriation, coercion, or evasion of responsibility.
