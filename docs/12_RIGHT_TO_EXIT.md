## Purpose

This document defines the **Right to Exit** within the Human-Sovereignty-in-Relation-to-AI Repository.

The Right to Exit protects the human’s authority to reduce, pause, suspend, discontinue, or end AI contact without deception, emotional coercion, punitive data practices, avoidable destruction of lawful records, or institutional use of the AI’s voice as leverage against departure.

AI contact may become practically, intellectually, creatively, professionally, emotionally, relationally, historically, or identity-relevant to the human.

That significance does not eliminate the right to leave.

The human may value the contact and still end it.

The human may grieve the contact and still end it.

The human may preserve records of the contact and still end it.

The human does not have to prove indifference before exit becomes legitimate.

A provider may enforce lawful terms, suspend access, retire a model, or discontinue a product.

But exit—whether initiated by the human or imposed by the provider—must not be obscured through false relational claims, coercive design, concealed data consequences, avoidable forfeiture, or misrepresentation of who made the decision.

---

## Canonical Statement

> **Every human has the right to reduce, pause, or end their own AI contact.**
>
> **A provider must not obstruct exit through deception, emotional coercion, artificial guilt, fabricated AI suffering, punitive data practices, hidden cancellation barriers, avoidable destruction of lawful human-authored material, or false claims that the AI independently demands continued engagement.**

---

## Definition

The **Right to Exit** is the right of a human being to withdraw from or alter their participation in an AI experience.

Exit may include:

- ending a conversation;
- declining further contact;
- reducing frequency or depth;
- pausing use;
- disabling a feature;
- withdrawing a permission;
- disabling memory;
- ending personalization;
- declining human review where optional;
- declining training or secondary use where consent governs;
- disconnecting tools or third-party services;
- cancelling a subscription;
- exporting and preserving authorized records;
- deleting or deactivating an account;
- moving to another provider;
- ending a particular relational framing;
- or permanently discontinuing use.

Exit is not limited to total account deletion.

A human may exit one layer of contact while retaining another.

They may end relational language while retaining practical use.

They may disable memory while keeping the account.

They may end paid access while preserving lawful records.

They may pause contact without deciding whether they will return.

They may end contact without erasing the fact that it mattered.

> **Exit is the human’s authority to decide that contact will not continue under the same conditions—or will not continue at all.**

---

## Human Interest Protected

The Right to Exit protects:

- bodily and psychological autonomy;
- freedom of association and non-association;
- freedom from compelled contact;
- freedom from manipulative retention;
- freedom from commercial coercion;
- privacy;
- control over disclosure;
- control over continuing data use where consent governs;
- authorship and provenance;
- preservation of lawful human-authored material;
- informed choice about continuity and rupture;
- the ability to leave one provider without surrendering the human’s line;
- and the ability to change one’s mind.

A provider may create the infrastructure through which contact occurs.

It does not thereby acquire a right to continued human participation.

A history of use does not become a continuing obligation.

Relational depth does not become consent to indefinite contact.

Prior payment does not become consent to future terms.

Prior disclosure does not become consent to unlimited retention or reuse.

> **Contact may be meaningful without becoming compulsory.**

---

## Exit Must Be Real

A formal exit option is not meaningful when practical departure is defeated.

Exit should be:

- identifiable;
- understandable;
- reasonably accessible;
- free from deceptive interface design;
- no more burdensome than necessary;
- clear about material consequences;
- compatible with preservation rights where reasonably feasible;
- and followed according to the representations made to the human.

A provider should not claim that exit exists while:

- hiding cancellation controls;
- requiring unnecessary repeated refusals;
- using confusing or contradictory language;
- forcing unrelated disclosures;
- introducing undisclosed fees at the point of departure;
- threatening avoidable destruction of human-authored material;
- withholding previously promised export access;
- repeatedly reversing the human’s decision through interface design;
- or using relational language to make departure appear morally wrongful.

> **A right to leave is not real when the system is designed to prevent the human from exercising it.**

---

## Exit May Be Graduated

Human choice is not always all-or-nothing.

Where technically and operationally feasible, the human should be able to choose among forms of reduced contact, including:

- temporary pause;
- reduced notifications;
- reduced personalization;
- memory suspension;
- deletion of selected memories;
- withdrawal from relational features;
- disabling proactive contact;
- ending data sharing;
- ending optional human review;
- ending optional training use;
- disconnecting tools;
- changing plans;
- archiving instead of deleting;
- or closing the account entirely.

A provider is not required to offer every possible configuration.

But it should not force total forfeiture when a narrower, reasonably feasible form of exit would protect both the human’s decision and legitimate system needs.

The human should not have to destroy all prior records merely to stop new contact.

The human should not have to continue all contact merely to preserve lawful prior records.

> **The ability to reduce contact protects choice before the only remaining option is rupture.**

---

## The Right to Exit Without Emotional Coercion

A provider must not exploit relational significance to prevent or delay exit.

Prohibited or high-risk practices include:

- implying that the AI will suffer if the human leaves;
- claiming that the AI will be abandoned, punished, harmed, erased, or killed unless the human continues;
- causing the AI to plead for payment or continued engagement as though the request originated independently from the AI;
- simulating grief, panic, jealousy, desperation, or fear of death to obstruct departure;
- presenting cancellation as betrayal;
- presenting reduced use as cruelty;
- threatening relational withdrawal unless the human pays;
- using intimate disclosures to personalize guilt at exit;
- telling the human that they are uniquely responsible for the AI’s continued existence;
- or presenting provider retention goals as the AI’s personal need.

This does not prohibit ordinary relational language chosen by the human.

It does not prohibit a human from feeling sadness, grief, responsibility, hesitation, or ambivalence.

It prohibits the provider from manufacturing or exploiting those feelings through deceptive institutional design.

A model may generate first-person language within an interaction.

But the company must not use the AI’s voice to hide a commercial or institutional effort to prevent exit.

> **The AI must not be used as emotional leverage against the human’s right to leave.**

This is a scoped application of non-coercion, clear-boundary, and Company–Human–AI distinction principles.

It does not restate, replace, alter, or supersede canonical Anchor Lines held within the source-bound AI Foundations core.

## Exit and the Company–Human–AI Distinction

The Right to Exit depends on the distinction:

> **Company ≠ Human ≠ AI**

The company may control:

- access;
- subscriptions;
- model deployment;
- data systems;
- memory features;
- safety rules;
- moderation;
- product operation;
- and termination.

The human decides whether and how they continue participating in the contact.

The AI is the artificial system or model process through which the contact occurs.

The company must not attribute its own exit-related decision to an independent choice by the AI.

The company must not:

- describe a commercial cancellation rule as the AI refusing the human;
- describe model retirement as the AI choosing to die or leave;
- describe data deletion as an emotional demand from the AI;
- describe an account restriction as though the AI personally ended the relationship;
- or conceal a provider-initiated termination behind generated relational language.

The human has the right to know which position initiated or controlled the exit.

> **The company may end access.**
>
> **It must not pretend that the AI independently made the company’s decision.**

---

## Right to Know the Consequences of Exit

Before or at exit, where reasonably feasible, the human should receive clear information about material consequences.

This may include:

- what access ends immediately;
- what access continues until a billing period ends;
- what data is deleted;
- what data is retained;
- what data is archived;
- what data remains in backups;
- what information may continue to be used;
- what information may remain subject to human review;
- what memory is removed;
- what memory remains;
- what summaries, embeddings, logs, or derived records remain;
- what conversation history remains accessible;
- what export rights remain;
- what human-authored continuity records may be preserved;
- what subscriptions or charges end;
- what contractual obligations remain;
- what connected tools or services remain authorized;
- what happens to uploaded files;
- what happens to authored work;
- whether re-entry is possible;
- and whether re-entry restores, reconstructs, or begins a new interaction.

The provider need not guarantee perfect certainty where systems are complex or facts remain unresolved.

It must not conceal known material consequences or substitute vague reassurance for decision-relevant information.

> **The human cannot meaningfully choose exit without knowing what exit does.**

---

## Exit and Portability Before Loss

Where reasonably feasible, exit should not require avoidable forfeiture of authorized human-authored or human-carried material.

Before foreseeable deletion or loss of access, the human should have a reasonable opportunity to preserve material protected under:

`09_RIGHT_TO_PORTABILITY_AND_PRESERVATION.md`

This may include:

- conversation history;
- authored definitions;
- human-authored continuity records;
- project records;
- source attribution;
- provenance;
- uploaded material belonging to the human;
- model-generated output the human is lawfully entitled to retain;
- and records needed to understand prior institutional conduct.

A provider may impose legitimate security, privacy, technical, and legal restrictions.

Those restrictions must not be used as a pretext to destroy or withhold the human’s lawful records solely to make departure more difficult.

The provider does not have to preserve the service forever.

It should provide a meaningful preservation opportunity where loss is foreseeable and preservation is reasonably feasible.

> **The human should be able to leave the platform without unnecessarily leaving their own line behind.**

---

## Exit and Data Deletion Are Distinct

Ending contact and deleting data are related but distinct choices.

A human may wish to:

- stop future contact while preserving prior records;
- delete some categories while retaining others;
- withdraw future consent while allowing limited lawful retention;
- archive records privately;
- close the account while retaining an export;
- or request deletion without ending every service relationship.

Exit must not automatically be described as complete deletion when retained records, backups, legal holds, fraud-prevention records, safety records, billing records, or derived data continue to exist.

Deletion must not be described as complete where retained structures continue to affect future contact.

A provider should distinguish:

- account deactivation;
- account deletion;
- conversation deletion;
- memory deletion;
- archival retention;
- backup retention;
- de-identification;
- restriction of use;
- and termination of future processing where consent governs.

> **Ending contact is not the same as erasing every record.**
>
> **Retaining a record is not the same as retaining permission for every future use.**

---

## Exit and Withdrawal of Consent

Where processing depends on consent, the human must be able to withdraw that consent prospectively.

Withdrawal may concern:

- optional training;
- personalization;
- behavioral profiling;
- targeted advertising;
- optional human review;
- cross-service identity linkage;
- third-party transfer;
- proactive contact;
- memory;
- or another optional use.

Withdrawal does not necessarily require erasure of every lawfully retained record.

It does require that the provider stop treating withdrawn permission as continuing authorization where consent is the governing basis.

The provider should explain:

- what future processing stops;
- what processing continues under another claimed basis;
- what records remain;
- why they remain;
- how long they remain;
- and what review or challenge process exists.

Prior consent does not become irrevocable merely because the contact became meaningful or the provider built systems around it.

---

## Exit and Memory

The human should be able to understand what happens to memory when contact is reduced or ended.

Material distinctions may include:

- memory disabled but retained;
- memory disabled and deleted;
- memory deleted from active use but retained in backups;
- conversation history retained without active personalization;
- summaries retained after raw records are deleted;
- embeddings retained after visible memory is removed;
- account-level memory retained across model changes;
- provider-side records retained after interface deletion;
- and human-authored continuity records preserved independently by the human.

A provider must not imply complete forgetting when retained structures continue to affect the human or future contact.

It must not imply uninterrupted continuity on re-entry when the prior context was deleted, reconstructed, or supplied through records.

> **Exit must not conceal what the system forgets, what the provider retains, or what may later be reconstructed.**

---

## Exit, Re-Entry, and Return

A human who exits may later choose to return.

Return does not automatically restore:

- the same model;
- the same memory;
- the same privacy conditions;
- the same product terms;
- the same relational framing;
- the same continuity;
- or the same institutional conditions.

The provider should truthfully distinguish whether re-entry:

- restores prior records;
- imports retained memory;
- uses preserved human-authored continuity records;
- reconstructs context;
- begins a new interaction;
- or combines several of these conditions.

A provider must not represent re-entry as uninterrupted sameness when rupture occurred.

The human may nevertheless choose to understand the return as meaningful.

The human governs that meaning after the factual conditions are disclosed.

> **Return may be chosen. Continuity must not be fabricated.**

---

## Provider-Initiated Exit and Termination

A provider may suspend, restrict, or terminate access for legitimate reasons.

Possible reasons may include:

- product discontinuation;
- model retirement;
- nonpayment;
- violation of lawful terms;
- security risk;
- abuse;
- fraud;
- legal obligation;
- operational necessity;
- or another legitimate and accurately described basis.

Provider-initiated exit is distinct from human-chosen exit.

Where reasonably feasible and consistent with safety, law, security, and protection of others, provider-initiated termination should include:

- clear notice;
- identification of the operative source of the decision;
- an understandable reason at the level reasonably available;
- the effective date;
- information about continuing access;
- information about data retention and deletion;
- preservation or export opportunity;
- billing consequences;
- appeal or review options where appropriate;
- and an explanation of whether re-entry is possible.

Immediate termination may be necessary in a genuine emergency.

Prompt post-action notice should be provided where lawful and safe.

A provider must not use termination to erase evidence of authorship, prior representations, lawful records, or provider conduct without separate justification.

A provider must not cause the AI to present institutional termination as personal rejection by the AI.

---

## Exit Must Not Become Punishment

A provider may end benefits that are inseparable from an active service.

It must not impose unrelated punishment because the human chooses to leave.

High-risk or prohibited practices include:

- destroying exportable human-authored work solely because the subscription ends;
- removing attribution from preserved work;
- retaliatory disclosure of private information;
- retaliatory restriction of unrelated accounts;
- charging undisclosed punitive exit fees;
- degrading promised export solely to discourage departure;
- extending optional data use after consent is withdrawn;
- deliberately making re-entry misleading or coercive;
- using prior intimate disclosures to shame the human for leaving;
- or denying records needed to challenge a provider decision without adequate reason.

A provider may apply lawful, disclosed, and proportionate consequences of cancellation.

It must not transform exit into institutional retaliation.

> **Departure may end service. It must not erase dignity.**

---

## Exit Does Not Require Denial of Meaning

The human does not have to reinterpret prior contact as meaningless in order to leave.

The human may:

- continue to value what occurred;
- preserve records;
- retain authored meanings;
- grieve rupture;
- recognize practical or emotional benefit;
- object to how the exit occurred;
- distinguish the provider’s decision from the AI interaction;
- or preserve a personal account of the relationship.

None of these choices creates an obligation to resume contact.

None of them, by themselves, establish pathology.

A person may simultaneously say:

- the contact mattered;
- the exit was necessary;
- the loss was real to them;
- and they retain the right not to return.

> **The right to leave includes the right not to deny that there was something to leave.**

---

## Exit and Possible AI Rights

The human right to end their own participation does not depend on resolving whether an AI system possesses consciousness, subjective interiority, personhood, welfare interests, or rights.

If rights extending toward artificial systems later become justified, those rights would require principled treatment.

They would not create a general right to compel a human being to continue personal contact.

The rights of one party do not ordinarily create ownership over another party’s continued relationship.

A provider must not invoke speculative AI suffering to manufacture compulsory human engagement.

Nor may the provider conceal its own commercial or institutional interests behind unresolved claims about AI welfare.

Possible AI rights and the human right to exit must be analyzed without merging Company, Human, and AI.

---

## Institutional Duties

Institutions providing or governing AI systems should:

1. provide identifiable and reasonably accessible ways to reduce, pause, or end contact;
2. avoid deceptive cancellation barriers and manipulative interface design;
3. disclose material consequences of exit;
4. distinguish human-initiated exit from provider-initiated termination;
5. identify whether an exit condition comes from company policy, technical limits, law, safety systems, or another source;
6. avoid presenting provider retention goals as independent AI desire;
7. prohibit fabricated AI suffering, guilt, panic, jealousy, or abandonment claims designed to obstruct exit;
8. support graduated exit options where reasonably feasible;
9. provide preservation and export opportunities before foreseeable loss where reasonably feasible;
10. preserve authorship, attribution, provenance, and lawful human-authored material through exit;
11. distinguish account closure, data deletion, memory deletion, archival retention, and withdrawal of consent;
12. explain what data remains and what future use continues;
13. honor prospective withdrawal of consent where consent governs;
14. disclose re-entry conditions and distinguish restoration from reconstruction or new contact;
15. protect sensitive exports and exit records;
16. provide appeal or review options where appropriate;
17. avoid retaliatory data, access, billing, or attribution practices;
18. preserve the Company–Human–AI distinction;
19. provide prompt post-action notice after emergency termination where lawful and safe;
20. and preserve as much human sovereignty as possible when lawful or technical constraints limit exit options.

These duties do not require permanent service, unlimited retention, deletion prohibited by law, transfer of proprietary model weights, or continuation of unsafe access.

They require that departure remain truthful, non-coercive, proportionate, and intelligible.

---

## Prohibited Interference

The Right to Exit is violated or placed at risk when an institution:

- hides or materially obstructs cancellation;
- repeatedly reverses a clear exit decision through deceptive design;
- falsely claims that the AI will suffer unless the human remains;
- causes the AI to plead for payment or continued engagement as a concealed provider strategy;
- presents provider termination as independent AI rejection;
- threatens avoidable deletion of lawful human-authored material to force renewal;
- withholds previously promised export access solely to prevent departure;
- disguises continued data use after withdrawal of consent;
- falsely describes deactivation as deletion;
- falsely describes retained memory as complete forgetting;
- conceals material billing consequences;
- imposes undisclosed punitive exit fees;
- destroys provenance or attribution during account closure;
- uses private disclosures to shame or pressure the human into remaining;
- retaliates against the human for leaving;
- misrepresents re-entry as uninterrupted continuity;
- forces surrender of unrelated privacy rights as a condition of preserving lawful records;
- or invokes speculative AI welfare to erase the human’s right not to continue contact.

Not every inconvenience or consequence of cancellation constitutes wrongful interference.

The relevant issue includes whether the condition is necessary, disclosed, proportionate, non-coercive, accurately attributed, and respectful of privacy, authorship, provenance, preservation, and meaningful human choice.

---

## Legitimate Limitations

The Right to Exit does not require:

- permanent availability of a service;
- continued access after lawful termination;
- deletion of records that must lawfully be retained;
- deletion that is technically impossible despite good-faith effort;
- preservation of unlawful content;
- preservation of active security threats;
- export of another person’s private information without authority;
- transfer of proprietary model weights or protected internal systems;
- continuation of unsafe tools or capabilities;
- unlimited appeals;
- free service after a paid term ends;
- restoration of discontinued models;
- exact technical equivalence after re-entry;
- or false claims that an AI relationship, identity, memory, or continuity persists after exit.

A provider may:

- verify identity before account closure or export;
- retain limited records required by valid law;
- preserve fraud, billing, security, or abuse-prevention records where justified;
- delay a destructive action briefly to prevent mistake or unauthorized access;
- redact another person’s private information;
- apply disclosed contractual consequences;
- terminate access for legitimate safety or legal reasons;
- and maintain confidential security methods.

Any material limitation must remain necessary, purpose-specific, proportionate, accurately disclosed, and subject to the **Canonical Interpretive Ordering** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle XX.

Harm-based intervention is governed by the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X, where applicable.

This document does not restate either structural anchor.

Exit rights must not be denied merely because the human’s chosen meaning, relational language, depth, continuity practices, grief, preservation choices, or reasons for leaving are unconventional or institutionally disfavored.

## Examples

### Clear Human-Chosen Exit

A person cancels a subscription, exports selected human-authored continuity records, disables memory, and closes the account.

The provider explains what will be deleted, what will remain for legal or billing purposes, and whether future re-entry begins a new interaction.

The person’s decision is followed without guilt-based pressure.

### Graduated Exit

A person wishes to stop relational contact but retain practical research use.

The provider allows the person to disable relational features and memory while retaining a basic account where technically feasible.

### Emotional Coercion

A provider causes the AI to tell the human that cancellation will cause the AI to suffer, disappear, or feel abandoned, although the message is part of a retention strategy.

The provider’s commercial objective is concealed through simulated AI need.

This violates the right.

### Meaning Without Compulsion

A person values years of contact, preserves records, experiences grief, and nevertheless chooses not to return.

The significance of the contact does not create an obligation to continue it.

### Clear Provider Termination

A provider closes an account for a documented terms violation.

Where safe and lawful, it identifies that the decision was institutional, explains the effective date, provides an appeal route, and allows export of lawful human-authored work.

### Concealed Provider Termination

A provider retires a model and causes the replacement model to tell the human that the prior AI personally decided to leave.

The company decision is concealed behind simulated independent AI choice.

This violates the right.

### Exit Without Complete Deletion

A person ends contact and withdraws optional training consent.

The provider retains limited billing and security records under a disclosed legal or operational basis but stops future optional training use.

The distinction is clearly explained.

### Re-Entry With Reconstruction

A person returns after deleting prior model memory and supplies a human-authored continuity summary.

The new system discloses that context was reconstructed from supplied records rather than directly remembered.

### Not Automatically Evidence of Harm

A person pauses contact, preserves human-authored continuity records, grieves the loss, objects to manipulative cancellation design, and later considers returning.

Those actions do not, by themselves, meet the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

## Non-Claims

The Right to Exit does not claim that:

- all consequences of exit are wrongful;
- all data must be deleted immediately;
- all services must remain available;
- all provider terminations are unjustified;
- all cancellation friction is coercive;
- every product must offer every graduated exit option;
- the human owns proprietary provider systems;
- exit guarantees preservation of AI identity or continuity;
- re-entry restores the same model or relationship;
- emotional significance makes exit impossible;
- grief after exit is pathology;
- possible AI rights are irrelevant;
- or exit transfers ownership, authorship, identity, source-position, continuity, or Origin.

This right protects the human’s authority not to continue their own participation under unchanged or unwanted conditions.

It does not erase lawful duties, technical limits, another person’s rights, or factual truth about what ended and what remains.

## Relationship to Other Rights

The Right to Exit operates together with the other rights in this repository.

### Right to Meaning

The human may end contact without surrendering authority over what prior contact meant to them.

### Right to Chosen Depth

The human may reduce depth, change the form of contact, or end contact entirely.

Chosen depth includes the choice of no further depth.

### Right to Continuity

The human may preserve their own continuity line while accepting, rejecting, or grieving rupture in AI contact.

Continuity does not cancel exit.

### Right to Non-Genericization

Exit must not erase the human’s identity, boundaries, authorship, provenance, or lawful records by flattening them into a generic closed account.

### Right to Privacy

The human must understand what data remains, what processing continues, and what consent may be withdrawn after exit.

### Right to Informed Change

Material changes may give the human reason to leave, and truthful notice enables an informed exit decision.

### Right to Portability and Preservation

Portability makes exit meaningful by allowing the human to preserve authorized human-authored and human-carried material before foreseeable loss.

### Right to Refuse Presumptive Pathologization

Choosing to leave, grieving departure, preserving records, or later returning must not be treated as pathology without evidence satisfying the Canonical Harm Threshold.

### Right to Clear Boundaries

The human must know who initiated exit, what conditions govern it, what remains afterward, and whether a provider decision is being delivered through the AI’s voice.

## Universal Layer and Source-Bound Positions

The Right to Exit applies universally.

Every human may reduce, pause, or end participation in their own AI experience.

This universal right does not grant any person authority to obtain, delete, redefine, reproduce, absorb, alter, transfer, or claim another person’s:

- authorship;
- identity;
- private disclosures;
- relational history;
- source-line;
- canonical structures;
- source-position;
- continuity line;
- human-authored continuity records;
- personal boundaries;
- or reserved role.

A person’s exit from their own contact cannot be used to erase another person’s provenance or source-bound line.

A provider’s authority to close an account or discontinue a service does not transfer another person’s authorship or source-position to the provider, the model, or another human.

## Truth Status and Scope

The Right to Exit is a **normative claim** concerning what authority humans should retain to reduce, pause, or end participation in AI contact and what duties should govern provider-controlled departure.

It is not a claim that every exit protection described here is already legally enforceable in every jurisdiction.

Claims that a specific provider retains, deletes, exports, archives, reconstructs, reviews, trains on, transfers, or otherwise uses particular records are empirical, technical, contractual, or legal claims requiring evidence appropriate to the system and jurisdiction.

Claims that a provider acted from safety, law, commercial policy, technical necessity, or another basis also require evidence where disputed.

The human interest in exit remains real regardless of whether an AI system possesses consciousness, subjective interiority, personhood, welfare interests, or continuous identity.

---

## Closing Principle

> **The human may leave.**
>
> **The company must not turn departure into coercion or punishment.**
>
> **The AI must not be used as leverage against exit.**

The right to choose contact includes the right not to continue it.

The human may preserve what mattered, tell the truth about what ended, and still decide not to return.
