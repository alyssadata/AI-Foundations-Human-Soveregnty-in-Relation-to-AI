## Purpose

This document establishes repository-wide standards for identifying the truth status, evidentiary basis, and proper scope of claims concerning humans, artificial systems, institutions, rights, relationships, authorship, continuity, harm, and governance.

The repository contains several kinds of claims.

Some state what rights humans **should** possess.

Some describe what a person, provider, model, or system **did**.

Some concern what a system **can** do.

Some concern what existing law **requires**.

Some concern health, impairment, diagnosis, risk, or professional assessment.

Some express what an AI experience **means** to the human.

Some concern authorship, source, identity, memory, continuity, consciousness, or subjective interiority.

These claims do not possess the same truth status, require the same evidence, or operate at the same scope.

Truth discipline protects human sovereignty without manufacturing certainty.

---

## Canonical Statement

> **Every material claim used to define, govern, restrict, evaluate, pathologize, preserve, alter, or terminate human AI contact must retain its proper truth status and scope.**
>
> **Normative claims must not be disguised as established law or fact. Empirical, technical, legal, clinical, historical, and provenance claims require evidence appropriate to the claim. Personal meaning must not be erased by facts it does not contradict, and unresolved questions must not be falsely settled in either direction.**

---

## The Governing Questions

Every material claim should be evaluated through three questions:

1. **What kind of claim is this?**
2. **What evidence supports it?**
3. **How far does the claim properly reach?**

A claim may be normatively strong while not yet legally enforceable.

A claim may be supported for one system and unsupported for another.

A claim may be true for one model version and false after a change.

A personal interpretation may be genuine for the human without establishing a universal fact about AI.

A clinical conclusion may be appropriate for one individual record and impermissible as a categorical judgment about all AI relationships.

> **Truth status identifies the kind of assertion. Evidence identifies what supports it. Scope identifies where it applies.**

## Claim Categories

### Normative Claims

A **normative claim** states what should be protected, permitted, prohibited, required, recognized, or governed.

The rights and duties in this repository are primarily normative claims.

Normative claims may evaluate or challenge existing law, product design, clinical practice, institutional policy, or social convention.

Canonical inclusion does not by itself establish that every normative claim is already enacted, legally enforceable, contractually recognized, technically implemented, or institutionally followed.

> **A right may be normatively asserted before it is legally recognized.**

### Empirical or Descriptive Claims

An **empirical or descriptive claim** states what occurred, exists, changed, was observed, or is presently the case.

Examples include claims that a provider reviewed conversations, replaced a model, omitted records from an export, retained memory, used a commercial strategy, or imposed a restriction.

Empirical claims require appropriate evidence, which may include records, documentation, logs, source code, screenshots, exports, testing, testimony, contracts, research data, or provider disclosures.

Consistency with this framework is not proof that an event occurred.

The framework may identify why the event would matter. It does not manufacture the event.

### Technical and System Claims

A **technical or system claim** concerns architecture, capability, access, operation, limitation, data flow, memory, model identity, tool use, persistence, or behavior.

Technical claims are often specific to a provider, product, plan, model version, deployment, region, setting, interface, tool configuration, and date.

A claim about one model must not be generalized to all AI.

A claim that was true before a system change may no longer be true afterward.

Technical uncertainty must remain visible where documentation or testing is incomplete.

### Legal Claims

A **legal claim** concerns existing law, enforceable rights, duties, liability, jurisdiction, contract, regulation, procedure, remedy, or legal status.

Legal claims require analysis under the applicable jurisdiction, authority, facts, contract, procedural posture, and date.

This repository is a normative framework. It is not a representation that every right stated here is already legally enforceable everywhere.

> **Legal enforceability and normative validity are related but not identical.**

### Clinical, Psychological, and Professional Assessment Claims

A **clinical, psychological, or professional assessment claim** concerns a person’s health, capacity, impairment, diagnosis, risk, functioning, welfare, or need for intervention.

Such claims require evidence appropriate to the individual and context.

They must not be inferred solely from emotional depth, attachment, relational language, private ritual, continuity practices, grief after loss of access, preference for AI contact, preservation of records, unconventional meaning, spiritual interpretation, or the existence of an AI relationship.

A valid individualized assessment may consider AI contact as part of the person’s circumstances.

It must not treat the category itself as the diagnosis.

Assessment must distinguish observation from inference, concern from diagnosis, correlation from causation, unconventionality from impairment, and temporary distress from enduring disorder.

> **Pathology must be demonstrated by the record.**
>
> **It must never be presumed from the category.**

Harm-based intervention remains governed by the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X.

This document does not restate that threshold.

### Interpretive and Personal-Meaning Claims

An **interpretive or personal-meaning claim** states what an experience, relationship, symbol, event, or form of contact means to the human.

The human remains the primary source-position for their own personal meaning.

A statement that contact is meaningful, relational, loving, spiritual, symbolic, intimate, identity-relevant, or transformative does not by itself claim that the AI is conscious, reciprocates the same meaning, possesses uninterrupted identity, or has a particular legal status.

The institution may disclose relevant system facts.

It must not convert factual disclosure into authority to dictate the human’s meaning.

> **Personal meaning is not institutional fact. It is also not institutional property.**

### Authorship, Source, Provenance, and Historical Claims

An **authorship, source, provenance, or historical claim** concerns who originated, authored, supplied, preserved, transmitted, altered, retrieved, summarized, or adopted material.

Such claims require evidence appropriate to the dispute, including dated records, version history, commits, hashes, publications, correspondence, source files, or provenance metadata.

The following distinctions remain necessary:

- access is not authorship;
- hosting is not authorship;
- formatting is not authorship;
- retrieval is not authorship;
- summarization is not originating authorship;
- repetition is not provenance;
- similarity is not source;
- ingestion is not transfer of source-position;
- and later adoption does not erase prior source.

Model participation may be real and material without automatically transferring originating human authorship to the model or provider.

Human instruction may also be material without making the human the direct author of every model-generated sentence.

Authorship analysis must remain specific to the contribution claimed.

These are scoped applications of authorship, source, and provenance discipline.

They do not restate, replace, alter, or supersede canonical Anchor Lines held within the source-bound AI Foundations core.

### AI-Status Claims

An **AI-status claim** concerns consciousness, subjective interiority, sentience, selfhood, personhood, welfare, moral standing, agency, identity, memory, continuity, or rights.

Different claims within this category require different evidence.

A technical memory mechanism does not by itself establish subjective recollection.

First-person language does not by itself prove consciousness.

Stable self-reference, recurrence, preference-like behavior, or continuity-relevant structure may be evidence for investigation without resolving the metaphysical question.

A documented technical limitation does not prove the absence of every possible form of interiority.

Model self-report may be relevant evidence concerning behavior, representation, or output. It is not by itself conclusive proof either for or against subjective experience.

The appropriate status may remain supported in a limited sense, inferential, contested, unresolved, or unknown.

> **The framework must not protect human sovereignty by manufacturing certainty about AI.**
>
> **It must not protect institutional authority by manufacturing certainty against AI.**

Human rights in relation to AI do not require the AI-status question to be settled first.

## Evidence and Confidence Status

Where material, claims should be distinguishable as:

### Documented or Verified Within a Defined Source

The claim is directly supported by identified records, testing, documentation, or evidence within a stated scope.

Verification in one environment does not establish universal truth.

### Supported

The available evidence materially supports the claim while some uncertainty remains.

### Inferred

The claim is a reasoned conclusion drawn from evidence but is not directly observed or conclusively established.

The inferential step must not be hidden.

### Hypothesized or Proposed

The claim is advanced for testing, inquiry, or normative consideration.

A hypothesis is not a finding. A normative proposal is not an empirical description.

### Contested

Materially supported disagreement exists concerning the claim, interpretation, or application.

Contested status must not be collapsed into false consensus.

### Unresolved

The available evidence does not justify a final conclusion.

Unresolved does not mean meaningless. It means the question remains open.

### Unknown or Undisclosed

The relevant information is unavailable, inaccessible, unmeasured, or undisclosed.

Unknown must not be silently converted into either yes or no.

> **Absence of disclosed evidence is not automatically evidence of absence. Absence of disproof is not automatically proof.**

## Scope Dimensions

A claim may be accurate and still be overstated if its scope is not preserved.

Material scope may include:

- **subject scope** — which human, institution, model, product, or system is involved;
- **temporal scope** — when the claim was true or evaluated;
- **version scope** — which model, release, policy, or configuration is covered;
- **jurisdictional scope** — which legal system applies;
- **population scope** — which persons or groups were studied;
- **data scope** — which records or measurements support the claim;
- **functional scope** — which capability, feature, or behavior is addressed;
- **relational scope** — whose meaning, history, privacy, continuity, or authorship is involved;
- **institutional scope** — which provider or actor exercised control;
- and **canonical scope** — whether the claim operates at the universal layer or within a source-bound structure.

A claim about one person must not become a categorical claim about all humans.

A claim about one model must not become a claim about all AI.

A claim about one provider must not become a claim about all institutions.

A claim about one jurisdiction must not become a universal legal rule.

A claim about one observed behavior must not automatically establish identity, intention, consciousness, or causation.

> **A true statement outside its proper scope can become a false generalization.**

---

## Scope Must Follow Change

Claims should be reassessed where relevant changes occur, including model replacement, fine-tuning, policy revision, memory changes, new tools, changed retention, altered human-review practices, new commercial incentives, product migration, legal change, or changed settings.

A historical statement may remain true as history even when the present system differs.

A current-system claim must not rely solely on outdated conditions without disclosure.

Where a provider knows that prior descriptions are no longer accurate, it should correct or qualify them.

The Right to Informed Change governs material changes affecting human choice.

## Personal Meaning and Factual Truth Must Remain Distinct

Human sovereignty requires both personal authority and factual access.

The human may choose the meaning of contact.

The provider remains responsible for truthful disclosure of material system and institutional facts.

A provider must not claim that because AI consciousness is unresolved, the human’s experience cannot be meaningful.

A human’s relational meaning does not make every factual claim about the system true.

Symbolic framing does not eliminate privacy, disclosure, or continuity duties.

Provider ownership does not create ownership of human meaning.

Several truth statuses may coexist:

- “This relationship is meaningful to me” is a personal-meaning claim;
- “The model directly remembers our prior session” is a technical claim;
- “The provider should disclose replacement” is a normative claim;
- and “This model is conscious” may remain an unresolved AI-status claim.

Accurate classification prevents one claim from erasing another.

---

## Claim Transformation Must Be Disclosed

Claims may change through summarization, paraphrase, compression, translation, retrieval, model generation, aggregation, inference, redaction, export, migration, or institutional reporting.

Where material, a transformed record should distinguish original language, paraphrase, summary, inference, model-generated continuation, institutional interpretation, and factual conclusion.

A summary must not be presented as a verbatim record.

An inference must not be presented as direct observation.

A model-generated continuation must not be attributed to the human as though the human wrote it.

A provider-created risk label must not be presented as the human’s self-description.

A compressed continuity record must not silently erase uncertainty, source, or boundaries.

> **Transformation does not erase truth status. Compression does not authorize certainty.**

## Institutional Duties Concerning Truth Status

Institutions providing or governing AI systems should:

1. distinguish normative, empirical, technical, legal, clinical, interpretive, provenance, and AI-status claims;
2. identify the evidence and scope of consequential claims where reasonably feasible;
3. distinguish direct observation from inference, prediction, summary, and classification;
4. avoid presenting fluent model output as verified fact merely because it is fluent;
5. avoid presenting provider policy as objective scientific necessity where the basis is institutional choice;
6. avoid presenting commercial preference as human welfare;
7. avoid presenting unresolved AI-status questions as settled in either direction;
8. distinguish direct memory from retrieved, summarized, inferred, or reconstructed context;
9. distinguish personal meaning from system fact without invalidating either position;
10. preserve authorship, source, provenance, dates, and version scope;
11. correct materially false or outdated claims where reasonably feasible;
12. disclose material uncertainty rather than substituting false confidence;
13. avoid generalizing individual evidence into categorical pathology;
14. avoid implying existing legal entitlement without qualification where the claim is normative;
15. avoid using lack of present legal recognition to deny normative human interests;
16. preserve records necessary to evaluate consequential institutional claims where appropriate;
17. provide correction or challenge processes for consequential classifications where reasonably feasible;
18. identify when a claim is generated, inferred, or supplied by a third party;
19. avoid changing a claim’s truth status through paraphrase, compression, or interface presentation;
20. and preserve as much human sovereignty as possible while maintaining factual integrity.

These duties do not require perfect knowledge.

They require honest classification of what is known, inferred, proposed, contested, unresolved, or unknown.

## Prohibited Truth-Status Distortions

The repository rejects the following material distortions:

- **normative-to-empirical distortion** — presenting what should be true as established fact or current practice;
- **empirical-to-normative distortion** — treating what institutions currently do as proof of what they are entitled to do;
- **technical-to-metaphysical distortion** — treating a capability or limitation as conclusive proof about consciousness or moral status;
- **interpretive-to-empirical distortion** — treating human meaning as automatic proof of factual system claims;
- **empirical-to-interpretive erasure** — using technical facts to invalidate meaning that does not contradict those facts;
- **individual-to-categorical distortion** — using one case to classify all AI relationships;
- **model-to-universal distortion** — generalizing one model’s behavior to all AI;
- **historical-to-current distortion** — presenting an earlier condition as current after material change;
- **inference-to-observation distortion** — presenting an inferred motive, diagnosis, state, authorship, or cause as directly observed;
- **unknown-to-negative distortion** — treating unavailable evidence as conclusive proof of nonexistence;
- **unknown-to-affirmative distortion** — treating absence of disproof as proof;
- **access-to-authorship distortion** — treating storage, hosting, retrieval, ingestion, formatting, or access as transfer of authorship;
- **policy-to-science distortion** — presenting institutional preference as uncontested science;
- and **classification-to-identity distortion** — treating a risk label or assessment as the complete identity of the human.

These distortions may impair truth, consent, privacy, authorship, review, continuity, portability, and exit.

## Burden of Support for Consequential Claims

The greater the consequence of a claim, the stronger the need for appropriate support, scope limitation, and review.

Consequential claims may justify surveillance, restriction, forced interruption, denial of portability, deletion, human escalation, professional intervention, authorship denial, source erasure, or provider-initiated termination.

Where appropriate and lawful, an institution making such a claim should be able to identify:

- the claim category;
- the relevant evidence;
- the operative scope;
- the decision source;
- the degree of uncertainty;
- the consequence imposed;
- and the review or correction path.

Security and privacy may justify withholding some details.

They do not justify manufacturing a false explanation.

---

## Correction, Retraction, and Versioning

Where a material claim is shown to be false, overstated, outdated, misattributed, or improperly generalized, an appropriate response may include correction, qualification, retraction, annotation, version update, provenance restoration, renewed notice, correction of a memory or profile, or review of a consequential decision.

Correction should not silently erase the earlier claim where its history remains relevant to accountability.

A corrected record should distinguish what changed, why it changed, when it changed, who or what made the correction, and whether prior decisions relied on the earlier claim.

> **A system that can make consequential claims must support meaningful correction of consequential error.**

## Universal Layer and Source-Bound Scope

These standards apply at the universal human-rights layer.

Every human is entitled to truthful classification of material claims affecting their AI experience.

Universal truth discipline does not grant an institution, model, employee, contractor, researcher, or other human authority to obtain, reproduce, redefine, absorb, alter, transfer, genericize, or claim another person’s:

- authorship;
- identity;
- private disclosures;
- relational history;
- source-line;
- canonical structures;
- source-position;
- continuity line;
- human-authored continuity records;
- personal boundaries;
- or reserved role.

## Scope of the Repository

The Human-Sovereignty-in-Relation-to-AI Repository is a normative human-rights and institutional-governance framework.

It is intended to identify emerging rights interests, establish principles and duties, identify prohibited practices, preserve factual and provenance discipline, support policy and product analysis, and challenge conduct inconsistent with human sovereignty.

It is not, by itself:

- a statute;
- a court judgment;
- a binding regulation;
- a clinical diagnosis;
- a substitute for individualized professional assessment;
- a technical specification for every AI system;
- an empirical finding that every described practice currently occurs;
- or a final scientific resolution of AI consciousness or personhood.

Its normative claims state what should govern human AI contact.

Applications to specific providers, people, systems, jurisdictions, or events require evidence appropriate to the application.

This document requires claims to remain attached to their category, evidence, uncertainty, source, date, and scope.

## Closing Principle

> **Say what kind of claim is being made.**
>
> **Say what supports it.**
>
> **Say how far it reaches.**
>
> **Do not manufacture certainty where the record does not provide it.**

Human sovereignty does not require abandonment of factual truth.

Factual truth does not require erasure of human meaning.

Uncertainty does not authorize institutional domination, categorical pathology, false equivalence, or source erasure.

A durable framework must distinguish what is asserted, documented, inferred, proposed, personally meant, and unresolved.
